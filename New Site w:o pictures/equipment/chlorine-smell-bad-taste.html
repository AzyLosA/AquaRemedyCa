Chlorine Smell & Bad Taste

If your water smells like bleach or has a chemical taste, you are likely noticing chlorine or chloramine — disinfectants added to city water systems.

These chemicals are necessary for safety, but they often affect how water tastes, smells, and feels in daily use.

What is in Los Angeles water?

The Los Angeles Department of Water and Power (LADWP) uses chloramine as its primary disinfectant.

Chloramine is a combination of chlorine and ammonia. It is designed to stay in the water longer than chlorine and protect it as it travels through miles of pipelines.

Source: LADWP Annual Water Quality Report

How much chlorine is allowed?

According to the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA):

  • Maximum level: 4.0 mg/L (ppm)

This is considered safe for drinking water, but many people can still notice taste and smell at much lower levels.

Source: U.S. EPA Drinking Water Standards

Why does your water smell?

  • Chloramine reacts with organic material in plumbing
  • Hot water increases the smell
  • Older pipes can intensify the taste
  • Standing water (overnight) becomes more noticeable

Is it safe?

Yes — municipal water is regulated and tested regularly.

However, safety does not always mean comfort:

  • taste and odor are not regulated the same way
  • many people are sensitive to chlorine
  • it affects shower experience and drinking water quality

How to test your water

  • Home chlorine test strips
  • Water quality reports from LADWP
  • Professional in-home water testing

What usually improves this problem

  • Carbon filtration systems
  • Whole house filtration
  • Reverse osmosis drinking systems

The right solution depends on whether the issue affects only drinking water or the entire home.
