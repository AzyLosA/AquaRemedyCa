Chlorine Smell & Bad Taste

If your water smells like bleach or has a chemical taste, you are most likely noticing chlorine or chloramine — disinfectants that are intentionally added to city water systems.

These chemicals are used to kill bacteria and help keep water safe while it travels through miles of pipes before it reaches your home.

But the same reason they work is also why people notice them. Chlorine is designed to disinfect water, and that can affect how water smells, tastes, and feels in daily use.

What is in Los Angeles water?

The Los Angeles Department of Water and Power uses chloramine, which is a combination of chlorine and ammonia.

Chloramine stays active longer than chlorine and helps protect the water as it moves through the distribution system.

In simple terms, the city adds it because it helps prevent bacteria growth inside the public water supply.

Source: LADWP Water Quality Reports

How much chlorine is allowed?

According to U.S. EPA drinking water standards:

  • 4.0 mg/L is the maximum allowed level
  • 4.0 ppm is the same measurement expressed as parts per million

So when you see mg/L and ppm in this context, they mean the same thing.

These levels are considered legally safe for municipal drinking water, but many people can still smell or taste chlorine at levels far below that maximum.

Why do you notice it more at home?

  • Hot water usually makes chlorine smell stronger
  • Water sitting overnight can taste or smell more noticeable
  • Older plumbing can change how the water tastes
  • Some people are simply more sensitive to chlorine than others

That is why one person may say the water seems fine while another immediately notices the smell.

Why is chlorine added if people do not want it?

Chlorine is added for one main reason: public safety.

It helps stop bacteria and other microorganisms from growing in the water while it moves through the city system. Without disinfectants, municipal water would be much harder to keep safe.

But once the water reaches your home, that job is already done. At that point, many homeowners prefer to reduce chlorine taste and smell for drinking, cooking, showering, and overall comfort.

Is chlorine good or bad?

Chlorine is useful as a disinfectant, but it is not something your body needs. It is there to control bacteria in the municipal system.

So the answer is:

  • It is useful for the city water supply
  • It is not added because it improves taste or comfort
  • Many homeowners choose to reduce it at home for better water quality

In other words, it is part of municipal water treatment — not part of better-tasting or more enjoyable water inside the home.

How can you test your water?

  • Home chlorine test strips
  • City water quality reports
  • Professional in-home water testing

A simple home test can often confirm whether chlorine or chloramine is present at a noticeable level.

What usually helps reduce chlorine smell and taste?

  • Carbon filtration
  • Whole house filtration systems
  • Reverse osmosis drinking water systems

The right solution depends on whether the problem is only in your drinking water or whether you also want better water for showering and the rest of the house.
