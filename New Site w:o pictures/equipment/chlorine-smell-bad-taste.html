Chlorine Smell & Bad Taste

If your water smells like bleach or has a chemical taste, you are noticing chlorine or chloramine — disinfectants that are intentionally added to city water systems.

These chemicals are used to kill bacteria and make water safe to travel through miles of pipes before it reaches your home.

But the same reason they work is also why people notice them — chlorine is designed to kill living organisms, and that affects how water smells, tastes, and feels in everyday use.

What is in Los Angeles water?

The Los Angeles Department of Water and Power (LADWP) uses chloramine, which is a combination of chlorine and ammonia.

Chloramine stays active longer than chlorine and protects water inside the distribution system.

Source: LADWP Water Quality Reports

How much chlorine is allowed?

According to U.S. EPA standards:

  • Maximum: 4.0 mg/L
  • Equivalent: 4.0 ppm

This level is considered safe, but many people notice chlorine at much lower levels.

Why you notice it at home

  • Hot water increases chlorine smell
  • Water sitting overnight becomes stronger
  • Plumbing affects taste and odor
  • Sensitivity varies from person to person

Is it good or bad?

Chlorine is necessary for safety — it prevents bacteria and contamination.

But it is not something your body needs. It is simply a disinfectant.

Once water reaches your home, the job of chlorine is already done. At that point, many homeowners choose to remove it.

How to test your water

  • Home chlorine test strips
  • City water reports
  • Professional testing

What helps reduce chlorine

  • Carbon filtration
  • Whole house systems
  • Reverse osmosis systems

The right solution depends on your home and how you use water.
