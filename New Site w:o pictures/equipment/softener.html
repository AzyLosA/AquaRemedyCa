Water Softener

Hard water is one of the most common problems in Southern California, and one of the most noticeable inside the home.

A water softener is designed to remove hardness minerals from water and protect your plumbing, fixtures, appliances, and daily comfort.

In the Los Angeles area, water hardness can vary a lot depending on location:

  • Burbank: around 15–17 GPG
  • Los Angeles: often around 10–18 GPG
  • Ventura County: up to 80 GPG

That is why some homes see light spotting, while others get heavy white calcium buildup very quickly.

What is hard water?

Hard water contains high levels of dissolved calcium and magnesium. When water dries, those minerals stay behind. That is why homeowners see white spots on faucets, scale on shower glass, and buildup around fixtures.

Over time, hard water does not just affect what you see. It also builds up inside pipes, water heaters, tankless heaters, valves, and appliances.

What problems does it cause?

White spots and calcium buildup

Hard water leaves scale on faucets, glass, tile, and fixtures.

Dry skin and rough hair

Mineral-heavy water can make showering feel harsher and leave residue on the body.

Soap and detergent work worse

Hard water reduces lather and makes soaps less effective.

Damage inside plumbing and appliances

Mineral scale slowly builds inside pipes, heaters, dishwashers, and other water-using equipment.

What does a water softener do?

A water softener removes calcium and magnesium through an ion exchange process. Inside the system, the hardness minerals are exchanged for sodium, which prevents scale from forming the same way.

In simple terms, the system changes the water so it no longer behaves like hard water. That means less buildup, better soap performance, and a noticeably better day-to-day water experience.

How does it work?

Water enters the softener and passes through resin media. The resin captures hardness minerals and releases sodium instead. Over time, the resin needs to recharge, so the system automatically regenerates using salt and sends the discharge to a drain.

That is why a water softener usually needs:

  • installation on the main water line
  • a nearby drain connection for regeneration
  • an electrical outlet
  • enough space for the system and brine tank

Where is it installed?

A whole-house softener is usually installed near the main water entry to the home. That way, the home receives softened water before it branches off to bathrooms, kitchen, laundry, and water heater.

Every home is different

There is no one-size-fits-all softener. The right system depends on:

  • your actual hardness level
  • how many people live in the home
  • how much water the home uses
  • pipe size and installation layout

Some homes need a smaller standard residential setup. Other homes need larger capacity systems, bigger pipe sizing, or a different layout altogether.

That is why we start with a water test and then prepare the right recommendation and estimate based on the house itself.
