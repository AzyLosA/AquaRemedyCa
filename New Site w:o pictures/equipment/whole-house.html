Whole House Filtration

Whole house filtration is designed to treat the water entering your home before it reaches your showers, faucets, laundry, kitchen, and appliances.

Instead of improving water at only one sink, this type of system improves water quality throughout the entire house.

Homeowners usually start looking at whole house filtration when they notice:

  • strong chlorine smell in showers or sinks
  • bad taste or odor in tap water
  • skin and hair irritation after showering
  • concerns about chemical exposure in daily water use

In many city water areas, disinfectants like chlorine or chloramine are added to the water supply. A whole house filtration system is one of the main ways homeowners reduce those unwanted effects.

What is whole house filtration?

A whole house filtration system is installed on the main incoming water line. That means the water is treated before it spreads through the rest of the home.

So if someone in the home takes a shower, washes hands, does laundry, or uses kitchen water, they are using filtered water instead of untreated water directly from the city supply.

Why do homeowners install it?

Chlorine smell in the shower

One of the most common complaints is that shower water smells like pool water or chemicals.

Skin and hair irritation

Many homeowners feel that filtered water is more comfortable for everyday bathing.

Bad taste and odor

Whole house filtration can improve the smell and taste of the water used around the home.

General concern about contaminants

Some homeowners simply want cleaner water throughout the house, not just at one faucet.

What does catalytic carbon help reduce?

Catalytic carbon is a high-performance media commonly used in whole house filtration systems. It is especially useful when homeowners want to reduce chlorine, chloramine, and chemical odors.

Common reductions

• Chlorine

• Chloramine

• Chemical taste and odor

• VOCs

Additional concerns

• Disinfection byproducts

• Organic contaminants

• Some pesticides and herbicides in trace conditions

• General whole-home water quality concerns

How does it work?

Water enters the filtration tank and passes through the media inside the system. As the water flows through, the media helps reduce unwanted contaminants before the water reaches the rest of the house.

In backwashing systems, the unit periodically cleans itself by flushing out trapped material. This helps maintain system performance over time.

Where is it installed?

A whole house filtration system is normally installed near the main water entry point into the home. The goal is to treat the water before it goes to the rest of the plumbing system.

Depending on the setup, installation may involve:

  • main line plumbing connection
  • appropriate pipe size matching the home
  • drain access for backwashing systems
  • enough space for the tank and service access

Every home is different

Whole house filtration systems come in different sizes and configurations depending on the needs of the property.

The right setup depends on:

  • the contaminants in the water
  • how much water the home uses
  • the size of the house
  • pipe diameter and plumbing layout

That is why a water test and on-site evaluation are important before pricing the system. It helps determine the correct equipment size, installation approach, and final estimate.
